Hello readers! Many of you have been kind enough to notice that it\u2019s been a while since I\u2019ve posted on the blog. And instead of allowing what has been one of the most fun projects I\u2019ve ever worked on come to an unceremonious end\u2014I figured we\u2019d send out one last email and GIF for old time\u2019s sake. <\/p>\n\n\n\n

So the news (likely expected) is that my cofounder James Gentes and I are putting a halt to StartupBend. The blog began six years ago with the express mission of broadcasting Bend\u2019s startup activity to the world. At that time the notion of launching and funding a startup outside a major metro area was pretty wild\u2014and yet here so many of us were, in the middle of Oregon, wanting to build companies and build lives. <\/p>\n\n\n\n

And while I feel a bit silly using this phrase, I feel like 561 posts later, we can say: mission accomplished. In the first year, we aimed to profile 100 startups in Bend\u2014a goal that then seemed ostentatious. But we hit that number and profiled so many more founders after that. <\/p>\n\n\n\n

The first post was titled “A Place Where You Can Fail,<\/a>” and talked about the importance of building a community that gave people the confidence to try out their ideas. In the ensuing years, we wrote about everything from tracking local fundraising<\/a> to records set at BVC<\/a> to lessons learned from founders <\/a>whose startups didn’t work. <\/p>\n\n\n\n

As a former news reporter, I cherished the opportunity to learn from so many creative, passionate people, those starting companies and those supporting the efforts. And as Bend has grown, so have the resources, expertise, and network for entrepreneurs here. There\u2019s more capital. There\u2019s more attention and support for products beyond tech. There’s a vibrant university and the OSU Co-Lab supporting students and local founders. <\/p>\n\n\n\n

There are multiple co-working spaces, more events than you can attend and most importantly, a true belief that you can start something here\u2014that maybe even our little town is a great place to start something. That’s a far cry from five or six years ago when simply stating your location as Bend signaled a red flag to investors. To be sure, we also still face some imperative challenges\u2014there\u2019s capital, but who does it go to? Founders of color and women continue to be significantly underrepresented. Oregon remains a hard place to raise big money without outside assistance, and the tech talent pool could always be larger. 
<\/p>\n\n\n\n

Even as I write this stuff down, I think there’s so much ground still to cover. So why stop? For me, the answer is more personal. In the years that I\u2019ve been writing the vast majority of this blog, I\u2019ve also been running my own business, supporting a spouse growing a startup, raising two elementary-aged kiddos, and generally burning the candle at all ends. Sometime around last summer, I just got tired. I\u2019d plan to blog and then not. I gave myself permission to take a break and then realized that I couldn\u2019t really start back up. I also felt a little worn out from living, breathing and chronicling startup life. It’s an exciting rollercoaster, but a rollercoaster nonetheless. <\/p>\n\n\n\n

I was a newspaper reporter for more than a decade. And in the best newsrooms, editors would rotate reporters off beats after five years. That\u2019s because inevitably what once was exciting seemed standard. You get a little cynical. You\u2019re less inclined to hear a pitch because you\u2019ve heard so many. I started feeling these things and recognized that I wouldn\u2019t do my best work blogging about startups anymore. It was time for something else for me\u2014and for someone else to take the reins. 

So all that said, what an exciting, inspiring and fun adventure this has been. I\u2019d be remiss if I didn\u2019t thank all the people who gave this blog their love and support. <\/p>\n\n\n\n
