Building a startup community is hard. It\u2019s usually a volunteer effort led by busy entrepreneurs who are passionate about making their city a place where their industry can thrive.<\/strong><\/p>\n

We discovered a way to grow our local network of founders, advisors, and investors by 500% in just a few months.<\/p>\n

Here\u2019s how we did it.<\/h2>\n

<\/p>\n

First, we identified industry \u2018clusters\u2019 that already existed within our community.<\/strong><\/p>\n

These might be obvious if your city is known for selling a particular type of product or service, but there are likely to be many small, innovative groups who are informally organized and are part of your ecosystem.<\/p>\n

Ed Morrison, a regional economic development advisor, defines clusters<\/a> as a group of members that \u201cshare goals of creating new value, are deeply committed to helping each other on specific projects, and share a common objective of regional transformation.\u201d<\/em><\/p>\n

<\/p>\n

In our case, our local economic development organization knew of four major clusters that were loosely organized:<\/p>\n

    \n
  • Tech (software & hardware)<\/li>\n
  • Bio-Science<\/li>\n
  • Outdoor Products<\/li>\n
  • Microbrew<\/li>\n<\/ul>\n

    If you aren\u2019t sure what clusters might exist, try searching meetup.com<\/a> for topics such as \u2018tech\u2019, \u2018science\u2019, or \u2018entrepreneur\u2019. Searching for \u2018tech\u2019 in Portland identifies nearly 40 groups, including NewTechPDX which has nearly 3,000 members.<\/p>\n

    <\/p>\n

    Find a meetup with a large number of members, then click through to look at the section labeled \u2018People in this Meetup are also in:<\/p>\n

    <\/p>\n

    Reach out to the organizers of the meetups you see there. You\u2019ll find they are often entrepreneurs, and many of the attendees are local professionals interested in supporting the community.<\/p>\n

    Why organize around industry clusters?<\/h2>\n

    Clusters are made up of professionals with experience in their industry, either as an investor, advisor, entrepreneur, or supporter. We’ve found every professional to be a potential advisor for others in the community.<\/p>\n

    Each cluster should be viewed as a small startup community. They have similar needs and can benefit from working together. Most of them are trying to provide these types of services for their cluster:<\/p>\n

      \n
    • Networking events<\/li>\n
    • Coworking space<\/li>\n
    • Startup incubation \/ accelerators<\/li>\n
    • Education opportunities for founders<\/li>\n
    • Mentorship from advisors in their industry<\/li>\n
    • Investors with knowledge of their industry<\/li>\n
    • News coverage \/ publicity<\/li>\n
    • Jobs \/ access to talent<\/li>\n
    • Membership programs & sponsors (revenue)<\/li>\n<\/ul>\n

      Collectively, these groups bring a focal point to the entrepreneurial activity within a community, and by bringing them together we’ve been able to:<\/p>\n

        \n
      • Meet monthly to discuss what issues are a priority for everyone<\/li>\n
      • Jointly pursue grants and other funding opportunities<\/li>\n
      • Cross-promote activities and share resources<\/li>\n
      • Have a single voice when interacting with local government<\/li>\n<\/ul>\n

        This sketch shows the basic startup ecosystem<\/a> that evolves around clusters:<\/p>\n

        <\/p>\n

        Once\u00a0you’ve\u00a0identified the clusters in your community:<\/strong><\/p>\n

        Find one or two representatives from each cluster and bring them together with a monthly meeting. Start by identifying the immediate goals of each cluster and where<\/p>\n

        Once we got rolling, we discovered more industries that were eager to get involved. Our list of clusters expanded to include 3 more:<\/p>\n

          \n
        • Energy<\/li>\n
        • Food & Beverage<\/li>\n
        • Robotics<\/li>\n<\/ul>\n

          Note that the investment community prefers high-growth<\/em> businesses that are likely to offer a 3 to 7 year return on their investment. This means the ecosystem is slightly skewed towards scalable, exponential growth startups rather than what many call \u2018lifestyle\u2019 businesses.<\/p>\n

          You can tell the difference by looking at the growth path<\/a> for each type:<\/p>\n

          <\/p>\n

          As Kauffman points out, the majority of job growth in America comes from new and young companies<\/a>, so linear companies will contribute to the economic growth of your city and should not be excluded, however they may not benefit from the investor network in your community.<\/p>\n

          The majority of job growth in America comes from new and young companies. @KauffmanFDN<\/p><\/blockquote>\n

          How do you know which companies have high-growth potential?<\/strong><\/p>\n

          We’ve found the best way to determine if a company has high-growth potential is by identifying whether they sell a product<\/strong>\u00a0or a service<\/strong>.<\/p>\n

          A product company has distribution potential (beer, software). A service company will usually focus on selling locally (realtors, accountants). Software as a Service (SaaS) companies create products that are distributed via the web and sold as a monthly subscription, often called a \u2018service\u2019.<\/p>\n

          How we measured the growth of our startup community:<\/h2>\n

          Rather than using Kauffman\u2019s description of entrepreneurial density<\/a>, which is:<\/p>\n

          Entrepreneurial density = (# treps + # startup or high growth co. employees) \/ adult population<\/p><\/blockquote>\n

          We decided to begin with a simple metric: # of startups<\/strong><\/p>\n

          We asked people in our community how many startups they could name. Generally people could list five to ten companies.<\/p>\n

          Our city ranked #16 in the nation<\/a> for entrepreneurial density by the Kauffman Foundation, so we knew there had to be more startups in our community:<\/p>\n

          <\/p>\n

          The clusters made it possible to identify even more startups than we originally anticipated were here. In fact, BendTECH’s #50startups project has uncovered at least 50 (a 5x times increase).<\/p>\n

          You will be amazed at how many startups you will find once you start digging.<\/p>\n

          The key is to organize them in a way to support each other.<\/p>\n

          Use industry clusters to coordinate the startups, advisors, and investors in your community.<\/p><\/blockquote>\n

          Clusters are natural networks for collaboration and provide a great starting point to build a thriving ecosystem. They led us to successfully raising $50k from our local city and a matching grant, which I’ll cover in a future post.<\/p>\n
